# Biochemistry — Core Concepts Overview

## What is Biochemistry?
Biochemistry studies the chemical processes that occur inside living organisms. It sits at the intersection of biology and chemistry. Everything a cell does — grow, divide, make energy, respond to signals — is driven by chemical reactions.

---

## 1. The Four Major Biomolecules (Macromolecules)

All life is built from four types of large molecules, each made of repeating smaller units (monomers).

| Macromolecule | Monomer | Main role |
|---|---|---|
| Carbohydrates | Monosaccharides (sugars) | Energy source and storage |
| Lipids | Fatty acids + glycerol | Membranes, hormones, energy storage |
| Proteins | Amino acids | Structure, catalysis (enzymes), signaling |
| Nucleic acids | Nucleotides | Information storage (DNA) and expression (RNA) |

---

## 2. Carbohydrates

Made of C, H, O — general formula (CH₂O)ₙ.

**Monosaccharides** — single sugars:
- Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) — primary fuel for cells
- Fructose — found in fruit
- Ribose — backbone of RNA

**Disaccharides** — two sugars linked:
- Sucrose = glucose + fructose (table sugar)
- Lactose = glucose + galactose (milk sugar)

**Polysaccharides** — many sugars linked:
- Starch — energy storage in plants
- Glycogen — energy storage in animals (liver and muscle)
- Cellulose — structural, plant cell walls; humans can't digest it

Carbs are broken down via glycolysis → energy (ATP).

---

## 3. Lipids

Hydrophobic (water-fearing) molecules. Not polymers — diverse structures.

**Fatty acids**: long hydrocarbon chains.
- Saturated: no double bonds, solid at room temp (butter, lard)
- Unsaturated: one or more double bonds, liquid at room temp (oils)

**Triglycerides**: 3 fatty acids + glycerol. Main form of fat storage.

**Phospholipids**: 2 fatty acids + glycerol + phosphate head.
- The phosphate head is hydrophilic (water-loving).
- Forms the lipid bilayer — the basic structure of all cell membranes.
- Arrangement: heads face water, tails face each other (inside).

**Steroids**: four fused carbon rings.
- Cholesterol — membrane component and precursor to steroid hormones.
- Hormones: testosterone, estrogen, cortisol.

---

## 4. Proteins

Most functionally diverse biomolecule. Built from 20 amino acids linked by peptide bonds.

**Structure hierarchy:**
1. **Primary** — sequence of amino acids (determined by DNA)
2. **Secondary** — local folding: α-helices and β-sheets (held by hydrogen bonds)
3. **Tertiary** — overall 3D shape of one chain (held by multiple bond types)
4. **Quaternary** — multiple chains assembled together (e.g., hemoglobin = 4 chains)

Shape = function. If a protein misfolds → it stops working or causes disease (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's involve misfolded proteins).

**Types of proteins:**
- Enzymes — biological catalysts (speed up reactions)
- Structural — collagen (connective tissue), keratin (hair, nails)
- Transport — hemoglobin (carries O₂ in blood)
- Signaling — hormones (insulin), receptors
- Defense — antibodies
- Motor — myosin (muscle contraction)

---

## 5. Nucleic Acids

Carry genetic information.

**DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid)**:
- Double-stranded helix
- Bases: A (adenine), T (thymine), G (guanine), C (cytosine)
- A pairs with T, G pairs with C (complementary base pairing)
- Located in the nucleus (eukaryotes)
- Stores the instructions for making proteins

**RNA (Ribonucleic acid)**:
- Usually single-stranded
- Base U (uracil) replaces T
- Types:
  - mRNA — messenger, carries code from DNA to ribosome
  - tRNA — transfer, brings amino acids to ribosome
  - rRNA — ribosomal, part of the ribosome structure

---

## 6. DNA → RNA → Protein (The Central Dogma)

The fundamental flow of genetic information in all cells:

```
DNA  →  RNA  →  Protein
   transcription  translation
```

**Transcription** (nucleus):
- RNA polymerase reads DNA and synthesizes mRNA.
- Only one strand of DNA is read (template strand).
- mRNA is a complementary copy of the gene.

**Translation** (ribosome):
- Ribosome reads mRNA codons (3-base sequences).
- Each codon codes for one amino acid (or start/stop).
- tRNA brings the matching amino acid.
- Chain of amino acids grows → becomes a protein.

**Genetic code**: 64 codons, 20 amino acids → multiple codons per amino acid (redundancy).
Start codon: AUG (also codes methionine).
Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA.

## 7. Enzymes

Enzymes are protein catalysts. They speed up chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy — the energy needed to start a reaction.

**Key properties:**
- Highly specific — each enzyme fits one substrate (lock and key model).
- Not consumed — reused many times.
- Sensitive to conditions — pH, temperature, inhibitors can deactivate them.

**Active site**: the region on the enzyme where the substrate binds.

**Induced fit model**: enzyme changes shape slightly when substrate binds (more accurate than lock-and-key).

**Enzyme kinetics (Michaelis-Menten)**:
- Vmax: maximum reaction rate when enzyme is saturated
- Km: substrate concentration at half Vmax — measures enzyme-substrate affinity

**Inhibition**:
- Competitive: inhibitor resembles substrate, blocks active site
- Non-competitive: inhibitor binds elsewhere, changes enzyme shape
- Allosteric: regulatory molecules bind at sites distant from the active site

---

## 8. Cell Membranes

The fluid mosaic model: a phospholipid bilayer embedded with proteins.

**Components:**
- Phospholipid bilayer — selectively permeable barrier
- Integral proteins — span the membrane (channels, receptors, transporters)
- Peripheral proteins — attached to surface (signaling, structure)
- Cholesterol — maintains membrane fluidity

**Transport across membranes:**
- Passive diffusion — small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) pass freely
- Osmosis — water moves from low to high solute concentration
- Facilitated diffusion — proteins help polar/large molecules cross (no energy needed)
- Active transport — pumps move molecules against gradient (needs ATP)
  - Example: Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase — pumps 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per ATP

## 9. Cellular Energy — ATP and Metabolism

**ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)** is the universal energy currency of cells.
- Energy is released when ATP → ADP + Pᵢ (phosphate group)
- Energy is stored when ADP + Pᵢ → ATP

**Cellular respiration** — the process of making ATP from glucose:

```
Glucose + O₂  →  CO₂ + H₂O + ATP (≈36-38 ATP per glucose)
```

Three stages:
1. **Glycolysis** (cytoplasm): Glucose → 2 pyruvate; net 2 ATP
2. **Krebs cycle / Citric acid cycle** (mitochondria matrix): pyruvate → CO₂; produces NADH, FADH₂
3. **Oxidative phosphorylation / Electron transport chain** (inner mitochondrial membrane): NADH, FADH₂ → large ATP yield via proton gradient

**Photosynthesis** (plants, algae, cyanobacteria):
```
CO₂ + H₂O + light energy  →  Glucose + O₂
```
- Light reactions (thylakoid): capture light, make ATP and NADPH, split water
- Calvin cycle (stroma): use ATP + NADPH to fix CO₂ into glucose

## 10. Cell Signaling

Cells communicate via chemical signals (ligands) that bind to receptors.

**Signal pathway (simplified):**
Ligand → binds receptor → receptor activates → intracellular cascade → gene expression or cell response

**Types of signaling:**
- Endocrine: hormones travel long distance via blood (insulin, adrenaline)
- Paracrine: signals act on nearby cells (neurotransmitters in synapse)
- Autocrine: cells signal themselves (common in immune cells)
- Contact-dependent: cells must physically touch

**Second messengers** — intracellular signals amplify the message:
- cAMP (cyclic AMP) — activated by many hormones via G-protein coupled receptors
- Ca²⁺ ions — trigger muscle contraction, enzyme activation
- IP₃ — releases Ca²⁺ from the ER

---

## 11. DNA Replication

Before a cell divides, DNA must be copied exactly.

**Semi-conservative**: each new double helix has one original strand + one new strand.

Key enzymes:
- **Helicase** — unwinds the double helix
- **Primase** — lays down a short RNA primer (starting point)
- **DNA Polymerase III** — reads template and adds complementary nucleotides (5'→3' only)
- **DNA Ligase** — seals gaps between Okazaki fragments (lagging strand)
- **Topoisomerase** — relieves tension ahead of the replication fork

Leading strand: synthesized continuously.
Lagging strand: synthesized in fragments (Okazaki fragments) then joined.

Error rate: ~1 mistake per 10⁹ bases (proofreading mechanisms correct most errors).

---

## 12. Mutations and Gene Expression Control

**Mutations** — changes in DNA sequence:
- Point mutation: one base changed (silent, missense, or nonsense)
- Insertion/deletion: frameshifts if not a multiple of 3
- Can be caused by UV light, chemicals, replication errors

**Gene regulation** — not all genes are active in all cells:
- Promoters and enhancers: DNA sequences that control when a gene is transcribed
- Transcription factors: proteins that bind DNA and activate/repress genes
- Epigenetics: chemical modifications to DNA or histones that alter gene expression without changing sequence (methylation = usually silences; acetylation = usually activates)

## Key Terms Quick Reference

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Anabolism | Building molecules (requires energy) |
| Catabolism | Breaking molecules down (releases energy) |
| Metabolism | All chemical reactions in a cell |
| Hydrolysis | Breaking bonds by adding water |
| Condensation | Forming bonds by removing water |
| pH | Measure of acidity; 7 = neutral; <7 acid; >7 base |
| Cofactor | Non-protein molecule needed by an enzyme |
| Coenzyme | Organic cofactor (e.g., NAD⁺, FAD) |
| Chaperone | Protein that helps other proteins fold correctly |
| Apoptosis | Programmed cell death |
